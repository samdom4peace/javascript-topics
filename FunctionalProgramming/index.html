<h2 class="w3-padding-16">Arithmetic Sequence</h2>
<hr>

<p>
An <b>Arithmetic Sequence</b> is a sequence in which <b>each consecutive term</b> is obtained by the
<b>addition of a constant value to a previous term.</b> <br>
OR <br>
An <b>Arithmetic Sequence</b> is a sequence whose <b>difference between consecutive terms is a constant value.</b> <br>
It is also known as an <b>Arithmetic Progression.</b> <br>
It is also known as a <b>Linear Sequence.</b> <br>
Because it is also known as a <b>Linear Sequence</b>, one can also define it as a sequence in which
the highest exponent of the number of terms in the general term ($nth$ term) is one. <br>
The next (succeeding) term of the sequence is found by adding a constant to the previous (preceeding) term
of that sequence. <br>
That constant is known as the common difference. <br>
So, the <b>common difference</b> is the constant that is added to a term of an Arithmetic Sequence in order
to give the next term of that sequence. <br><br>
<u>Example 1:</u> In the sequence: $3, 10, 17, 24, ...$ <br>
First term, $a = 3$ <br>
Second term = $10$ <br>
Third term = $17$ <br>
Fourth term = $24$ <br><br>
<i>
Teacher: how did we obtain the: <br>
second term from the first term? <br>
third term from the second term? <br>
fourth term from the third term?
</i>
<br><br>
$
2nd\:\: term - 1st\:\: term = 10 - 3 = 7 \\[3ex]
3rd\:\: term - 2nd\:\: term = 17 - 10 = 7 \\[3ex]
4th\:\: term - 3rd\:\: term = 24 - 17 = 7 \\[3ex]
$
So, we have a common difference ("common" because it is a constant value, "difference" because it is
the result of a subtraction procedure). <br>
So, the common difference, $d = 7$ <br><br>
<i>
Teacher: Does it make sense? <br>
What would be the fifth term?
</i>
<br><br>

<u>Example 2:</u> In the sequence: $-3, -10, -17, -24, ...$ <br>
First term, $a = -3$ <br>
Second term = $-10$ <br>
Third term = $-17$ <br>
Fourth term = $-24$ <br><br>
<i>
Teacher: how did we obtain the: <br>
second term from the first term? <br>
third term from the second term? <br>
fourth term from the third term?
</i>
<br><br>
$
2nd\:\: term - 1st\:\: term = -10 - (-3) = -10 + 3 = -7 \\[3ex]
3rd\:\: term - 2nd\:\: term = -17 - (-10) = -17 + 10 = -7 \\[3ex]
4th\:\: term - 3rd\:\: term = -24 - (-17) = -24 + 17 = -7 \\[3ex]
$
Here, the common difference, $d = -7$ <br><br>
<i>
Teacher: What would be the fifth term? <br>
What would be the seventieth term? <br>
Student: That would be a lot of work <br>
Is there an easier way to find it? <br>
Teacher: yes of course! &#x263A;&#x263A;&#x263A;
</i>
<br><br>

Back to <b>Example 1</b> (as it would be easier to demonstrate what we want to do) <br>
We already know that: <br>
first term = $a$ <br>
common difference = $d$ <br><br>
So, we have: <br><br>
$
1st\:\: term = a = 3 \\[3ex]
2nd\:\: term = 3 + 7 = 10 = a + d \\[3ex]
3rd\:\: term = 10 + 7 = 17 = a + d + d = a + 2d \\[3ex]
4th\:\: term = 17 + 7 = 24 = a + d + d + d = a + 3d \\[3ex]
$
<i>
Teacher: In that sense and using Algebra only (forget Arithmetic (the results/numbers) now),
because we want to come up with a formula <br>
What are the: <br>
$5th, 6th, 7th, 10th, nth$ terms?
</i>
<br><br>
$
5th\:\: term = a + 4d \\[3ex]
6th\:\: term = a + 5d = a + (6 - 1)d \\[3ex]
7th\:\: term = a + 6d = a + (7 - 1)d \\[3ex]
10th\:\: term = a + 9d = a + (10 - 1)d \\[3ex]
nth\:\: term = a + (n - 1)d \\[3ex]
nth\:\: term = a + d(n - 1) \\[3ex]
$

<b><u>To Determine:</u></b> If the Sequence is a Quadratic Sequence <br><br>

$

= 3rd - 2(2nd) + 1st \\[3ex]
= 1st + 3rd - 2(2nd) \\[3ex]
Also: \\[3ex]
4th - 3rd - (3rd - 2nd) \\[3ex]
= 4th - 3rd - 3rd + 2nd \\[3ex]
= 4th - 2(3rd) + 2nd \\[3ex]
= 2nd + 4th - 2(3rd) \\[3ex]

<h2 class="w3-padding-16">Recursive Sequence</h2>
<hr>
<p>
A <b>Recursive Sequence</b> is a sequence in which each <b>consecutive term</b> is obtained from one or more previous
terms based on an equation/relation. <br>
It is also known as a <b>Recurrence Sequence.</b> <br>
A recursive or recurrence sequence is defined by a <b>Recurrence Relation</b> or a <b>Recurrence Equation.</b> <br>
A <b>Recurrence Relation</b> is an equation that defines a recursive sequence. <br>
There are several types of recurrence sequences. <br>
We shall begin with Linear Homogeneous Recurrence Relations. <br>
A <b>Linear Homogeneous Recurrence Relation</b> of <b>degree: <i>x</i></b> is a relation of the form: <br><br>
$
RS_n = c * RS_{n - 1} + d * RS_{n - 2} + e * RS_{n - 3} + f * RS_{n - 4} + ... + p * RS_{n - x} \\[3ex]
where: \\[3ex]
RS_n = nth\;\;term\;\;of\;\;the\;\;linear\;\;recurrence\;\;relation \\[3ex]
RS_{n - 1} = term\;\;before\;\;the\;\;nth\;\;term \\[3ex]
RS_{n - 2} = term\;\;before\;\;the\;\;term\;\;before\;\;the\;\;nth\;\;term \\[3ex]
RS_{n - x} = term\;\;of\;\;the\;\;degree \\[3ex]
x = degree \\[3ex]
x = number\;\;of\;\;terms\;\;before\;\;the\;\;nth\;\;term \\[3ex]
c, d, e, f, ..., p = constants \\[5ex]
$

(2.)\;\; FS_n = \dfrac{\sqrt{5}}{5}\left[\left(\dfrac{1 + \sqrt{5}}{2}\right)^n - \left(\dfrac{1 - \sqrt{5}}{2}\right)^n\right] \\[7ex]
(3.)\;\; FS_n = \dfrac{\sqrt{5}}{5}\left[\phi^n - \left(\dfrac{1 - \sqrt{5}}{2}\right)^n\right] \\[7ex]
(4.)\;\; SFS_n = \dfrac{\sqrt{5}}{5}\left[\dfrac{\phi^3(\phi^{n - 1} - 1) + [(\phi - 1)(1 - \phi^2)][(1 - \phi)^{n - 1} - 1]}{\phi(\phi - 1)}\right] + 1 \\[7ex]

$
</p>
